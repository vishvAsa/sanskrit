People keep saying "Tamil is 60% Sanskrit," or "Malayalam is 80% Sanskrit," or "Kannada is 80% Sanskrit," as if it means something. This is clearly not linguistics, but mere vibes with percentages.

Let's see why. 🧵

English vocabulary is 60% French and Latin. Does anyone call English a Romance language? No, because that's not how language classification works. You classify by grammar, not by how many fancy words you borrowed from your neighbor. Grammar is the DNA of a language.

Japanese has 40-60% Chinese-origin words. Persian is stuffed with Arabic. Nobody calls Japanese "Sino-Tibetan" or Persian "Semitic." Because borrowing words is like borrowing clothes, you're still you underneath.

The gold standard textbook on language contact made this crystal clear: lexical borrowing is easy and casual. Structural borrowing is rare and requires deep, prolonged, intense contact.

When you find borrowed grammar, something serious has happened. Everyone talks about Sanskrit words going into Tamil, Malayalam, or Kannada. But no one talks about what Dravidians contributed to Sanskrit.

If we do a quick research from an academic perspective on what Dravidian has given to Sanskrit, or should we call it Prakrit? It's not merely words; it's the grammar, the skeleton of the spoken avrieties of Prakrit languages.
Retroflection, one of the classic sounds, those ṭ, ḍ, ṇ sounds that make Indian languages sound "Indian"? Proto-Indo-European didn't have them; Greek and Latin don't either, nor does Old Persian. None of the Germanic or Slavic branches have them. Not a single branch outside South Asia has them systematically. What does this tell us, where did Sanskrit get a whole retroflex consonant series ṭ, ṭh, ḍ, ḍh, ṇ, ṣ that participates productively in morphophonemic alternations?

Scholars such as Emeneau and Burrow argued the only possible way as from Dravidian, where retroflexion is reconstructible all the way back to Proto-Dravidian. Hock tried to argue some retroflexes could have developed internally in Indo-Aryan branch. Fair enough for a few cases but even Hock admitted that the systemetic productivity of retroflexion showing up in environments where no internal rule predicts it points to contact influence.

The retroflex lateral /ḷ/ found in Vedic Sanskrit has no Indo-European source whatsoever. It points directly and exclusively to a Dravidian model. There is simply no other explanation on the table.

SOV word order of Dravidan structure, with the verb sitting at the end of every sentence. Every Dravidian language does this as its canonical pattern. Classical Sanskrit does this rigidly. But how and where did Sanskrit pick it from? from Proto-Indo-European?

PIE, reconstructed as having a relatively free word order, even though a preference for verb-final word order existed; the shift to SOV in South Asia, whereas SVO in Germanic or Romance couldn't be dismissed as just a natural evolution, but should be considered with an environmental trigger.

It's not verb-position development; Sanskrit developed postpositions rather than prepositions. Relative clauses before the head noun or the Genitive before the possessed noun. This is a full typological package from the Dravidian profile. The close cousins of Indo-Aryan languages, none of the Iranian languages underwent this same typological shift. If it were just natural Indo-European development, Iranian should have done it too.

The quotative construction is a deeply Dravidian syntactic pattern that uses a "say" verb to form reported speech and complement clauses. For instance, Tamil uses eṉṟu, Kannada uses endu, and Telugu uses ani.
Hindi has something similar, ki, with comparable forms everywhere, its not just a natural evolution. Sanskrit wholesale borrowed a complete way of building complex sentences, a syntactic borrowing from Davidian. Scholars like Masica identified this as a diagnostic Dravidian areal feature calqued into Indo-Aryan.

This is what linguists call structural borrowing par excellence. Not a noun, an adjective, or some fancy lexical borrowing from the surface-level contact.

A syntactic construction that reshaped how an entire language family handles reported speech. That's not cosmetic, that's surgery in my opinion.
A unique feature within Dravidian languages called "echo words", Tamil says puli-gili (tigers and such). Kannada has oota-giita (food and stuff). Replacing the first consonant with a random consonant to get a vague "etc." meaning. This is highly productive in most of the Dravidian languages.

Hindi has khānā-vānā (food and stuff), cāy-vāy (tea and such), pānī-vānī (water, etc.). The same construction and logic are used by Hindi. This pattern exists nowhere else in Indo-European, not in the Iranian neighbors.

Oh, where did Hindi developed it from? Guess what, Dravidian again. The typological features don't stop here.

Hindi can formulate a sentence like "mere pās billī hai", this sentence formation couldn't be explained in other branches of the larger Indo-European, but one can very well explain it with Dravidian.
PIE was a fusional language, similarly Vedic Sanskrit, which also started as fusional. But watch what happened over time: the Prakrits, and then the New Indo-Aryan languages, drifted toward agglutinative morphology, postpositional case marking, and compound verb constructions.

What does this mean? The only explanation is that they drifted toward the Dravidian typological profile.
So far, I haven't mentioned one time about surface-level lexical borrowing from Dravidian languages into Vedic Sanskrit or other Prakrit varieties.

It is well understood that Rgveda has borrowed words from Dravidian even from the earliest stratum. Kuiper identified about 383 words that cannot be etymologized from PIE or PII. I wouldn't want to go into the discussion if they were borrowed from Dravidian, Munda, Sino-Tibetan, or the famous Language X. But, conclusively, some words were borrowed from Dravidian, proving an earlier contact with Vedic Sanskrit speakers.

What happened in the South Asian or the Indian context was two different borrowings. Dravidian languages borrowed Sanskrit "Lexical Items," including nouns, adjectives, and literary and religious terminology.

Sanskrit absorbed Dravidian "Structure", including phonology, syntax, morphology, and typological features.
