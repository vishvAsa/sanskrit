Lahore was one of the leading publishers of Sanskrit literature prior to partition and could have been a major center of Indological studies just like Varanasi, Pune, New Delhi, Mysuru, Kanchipuram, Kolkata, etc.

The Oriental College of the University of the Punjab was one of the leading institutions for Sanskrit studies, comparative philology, Prakrit studies, etc.

The Motilal Banarsidass publishing house, which is today the largest and most prominent publisher of Indological works, was originally founded in Lahore in 1903 by Lala Motilal Jain.

The original MLBD Lahore shop was burnt down in 1947.

The Jain family had previously established another branch in Patna in 1937 at the suggestion of Dr. Rajendra Prasad, so they shifted to Patna before settling in Varanasi in 1951.

Varanasi seemed like an obvious choice at first, since it was the heart of Indological scholarship at the time.
It was the site of Sampurnanand Sanskrit University, Banaras Hindu University, Chowkhamba, etc. and had libraries with manuscripts from across the subcontinent and a steady supply of scholars from all of over India who could edit and translate Sanskrit texts.

However, Motilal Banarsidass later moved to New Delhi in 1958 in order to expand their international exports since New Delhi had better infrastructure, proximity to national academic institutions, etc. 

The Vishveshvaranand Vedic Research Institute (VVRI), famous for publication of Mahāsubhāṣitasaṅgraha and the Śāntakuṭī Vaidika Granthamālā (Vaidika-Padānukrama-Koṣa), was also forced to evacuate Lahore.

The city was engulfed in communal violence and arson was frequently used as a weapon, especially in Hindu and Sikh areas (cf. Great Fire of Shah Alami), so their research institute was at risk of being burnt down.

The Pakistan government had even imposed an official ban which prevented the removal of its research library, manuscripts, and administrative records.
Nonetheless, workers managed to extract roughly 4,000 maunds of materials, piece by piece.

They relocated from Lahore to Hoshiarpur, where Shri Dhani Ram Bhalla granted the institute the use of his property (Sadhu Ashram).
He later formally donated it to the institute in 1956.
VVRI is still based in Hoshiarpur to this date.

For Pakistanis to boast about the Woolner Collection or the Bakhshali Manuscript or Gilgit Buddhist Manuscripts or figures like Pāṇini and Cāṇakya is profoundly hypocritical.
